_A DINO-pretrained ViT-S matches a pretrained ResNet-50 at predicting steering angle from a single dashcam frame, on as few as ~5,000 frames. The "transformers need big data" verdict turns out to be an artifact of how people adapt them, not the architecture._

> **Quick summary:** Vision Transformers are widely believed to be too data-hungry to compete with CNNs on small datasets. We tested that on end-to-end steering &mdash; predicting the steering-wheel angle from one forward camera frame, on a small slice (~5&ndash;16k frames) of the public comma2k19 driving dataset. A DINO-pretrained ViT-S, fully fine-tuned at a low learning rate, matches a pretrained ResNet-50 on turn-slice correlation (0.964 vs 0.967 at 16k frames, a gap smaller than the seed-to-seed spread). It matches the CNN; it does not beat it. And the result is conditional: the defaults a practitioner reaches for first &mdash; a frozen linear probe and horizontal-flip augmentation &mdash; reproduce the "ViTs don't work at small scale" conclusion. Competitiveness shows up only after those choices are corrected. This is an open-loop prediction study on a small, highway-dominant slice, not a driving system.

<!--truncate-->

The belief that ViTs need large datasets steers practitioners away from pretrained transformers whenever labelled data is scarce. If that belief is wrong, you have another strong backbone on the table for small-data problems. And the choices that made the difference here transfer beyond driving: full fine-tune over a frozen linear probe, no flip augmentation on an imbalanced target, and a diagnostic that catches model collapse. They apply to other small-data, imbalanced regression tasks.

## The task, and the hard part

End-to-end steering maps a camera image directly to a control output. It dates to PilotNet and underlies production stacks like comma.ai's openpilot. It is also a clean test for the data-hungry question, because driving logs are large but the labelled slice you actually train on is often small. Our task: predict the per-frame steering-wheel angle from a single forward camera frame, on 188 one-minute segments of comma2k19 (~33h of California highway driving, MIT-licensed). We split by route, never by individual segment, because consecutive segments of one drive are near-duplicates and a naive split leaks them across train and test.

The hard part is imbalance. Highway steering is overwhelmingly gentle: ~94.5% of frames sit within &plusmn;15&deg; of straight, leaving only ~5.5% on the turn slice. A model can score well on average by always predicting "straight," so average error hides whether it learned anything. The signal lives in the rare turns.

## Reach for the ViT the obvious way and it looks data-hungry

A frozen linear probe over the ViT's pretrained features gives turn-slice correlation 0.38. The standard, label-correct horizontal-flip augmentation collapses the model to a constant predictor. Both are the first things a practitioner tries, and both reproduce the "transformers don't work at small scale" story. The conclusion is real; it is just downstream of the adaptation, not the architecture.

The cleanest way to see this is to hold the architecture fixed and change only how it is adapted. Same DINO-pretrained ViT-S backbone, three adaptations:

A frozen probe reads as "the transformer can't capture steering." A full fine-tune at too-high a learning rate underfits. Only the low-learning-rate full fine-tune reaches conv-competitive quality &mdash; and it gets there on adaptation alone, without touching the architecture.

## The configuration we found necessary

Four choices, each ablated. We report them as a validated configuration for this task, not a tuned or transferable recipe.

1. **Full fine-tune at a low learning rate, not a linear probe.** Fine-tune the entire backbone (ViT: AdamW at lr 3&times;10&#8315;&#8309;). A frozen probe gives 0.38; a full fine-tune at lr 10&#8315;&#8308; underfits; only the low-LR full fine-tune reaches CNN-competitive quality (0.964). This single choice is what moves the pretrained ViT from sub-conv to conv-competitive.
2. **A cost-sensitive loss so the mean is not optimal.** On a ~95%-near-zero target, a plain loss rewards predicting the mean. We use a cost-sensitive smooth-&#8467;1 loss with weight _w_ = 1 + |y|/15, up-weighting turn frames so collapse to the mean is no longer loss-optimal.
3. **Evaluate scale-free, on the slice that matters.** Overall MAE flatters trivial predictors and R&sup2; is misleading here. We lead with turn-slice MAE, Pearson and Spearman correlation (overall and on turns), median absolute error, and `pred_sd` (the standard deviation of predicted angles). A degenerate mean-predictor has `pred_sd` near 0, so the diagnostic catches collapse that aggregate error hides.
4. **Drop flip augmentation on imbalanced targets.** Horizontal flip is label-correct, but it symmetrizes the already-~95%-near-zero distribution around 0 and reinforces predicting zero. We do not use it.

## The result: the ViT matches the CNN

With that configuration, the pretrained ViT matches the pretrained ResNet. At 16k frames, turn-slice correlation is 0.964 (ViT) vs 0.967 (ResNet), a difference of 0.003, smaller than the seed-to-seed std of ~0.006. Turn-slice correlation is already near-ceiling (~0.96) by 5k frames for both, so the ViT does not need more data to "catch up." We report the observed difference and its spread and do not run a formal equivalence test. The ViT matches the CNN here; it does not beat it.

For context: from-scratch PilotNet reaches only 0.87 turn correlation. And a trivial predict-previous-angle baseline (output the last frame's angle) has overall MAE ~0.45&deg; and turn-MAE ~1.4&deg;, which no learned single-frame model beats on overall error. It is a reference, not a target, and is not available to a single-frame model. The learned models' value is in the turn slice, not in overall error.

## The augmentation that backfires (and the diagnostic that caught it)

On this imbalanced target, the standard horizontal flip is harmful. With flip on, models collapse to a constant: `pred_sd` &rarr; 0 and correlation &rarr; 0, despite a plausible-looking training loss. Removing the flip (together with the cost-sensitive loss) recovered a genuine model: PilotNet turn-slice correlation went from 0 to 0.87. The loss and augmentation changes are entangled in this single recovery, so we credit the pair, not either alone.

<FlipCollapseDiagram />

The `pred_sd` diagnostic is what surfaced the collapse: strong models sit at `pred_sd` ~2.6&ndash;3.5 against a true signal of ~5&deg;, collapsed runs at ~0. Average error alone would have hidden it.

## Where this does not work

This is a deliberately small feasibility study, and we are explicit about what it does not show.

- **Open-loop only.** We measure per-frame prediction error, not closed-loop driving. Good open-loop error does not imply driving competence; errors compound under covariate shift. We make no claim that any model here would drive.
- **Small scale.** Minutes to hours of data, one chunk, one vehicle, highway-dominant. Findings may not hold at larger scale or off-highway.
- **Pretrained pipelines, not architectures.** The ViT is DINO-pretrained (self-supervised) and the ResNet is ImageNet-pretrained (supervised), so this compares pretrained pipelines, not architectures in isolation.
- **Near-ceiling caveat.** "Not data-hungry" is shown only at the two sizes we tried (5.2k, 16k), where the turn-slice metric is already near-ceiling, so we cannot rule out that the slice is simply easy at these scales.
- **Asymmetric sweep.** The adaptation sweep was run for the ViT, not symmetrically for the ResNet, so "competitiveness depends on adaptation" is a claim about the ViT, not a controlled architecture verdict. Some of our earlier ViT failures traced to infrastructure and code bugs (a silent CPU fallback, a loss-broadcasting bug), not only to adaptation; the numbers here are from the corrected GPU runs.

## Takeaways

- On small data, how you adapt a pretrained backbone can matter more than which backbone you pick. The same ViT-S went from 0.38 to 0.964 turn correlation on adaptation alone.
- Prefer a low-LR full fine-tune over a frozen linear probe when labelled data is scarce. The probe is what makes a pretrained ViT look data-hungry.
- Check augmentations against your label distribution. A label-correct augmentation (horizontal flip) can be actively harmful on an imbalanced target.
- Evaluate on the slice that carries the signal, not the easy bulk. Aggregate error hides collapse; a dispersion diagnostic like `pred_sd` catches it.
- A "data-hungry" verdict for an architecture can be an artifact of default adaptation choices. Correct the adaptation before concluding the architecture failed.
